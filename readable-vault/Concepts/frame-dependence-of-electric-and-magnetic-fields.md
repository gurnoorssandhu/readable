A central lesson of special relativity — already encoded in Maxwell's equations — is that electric and magnetic fields are not absolute: their values depend on the observer's reference frame.

**Illustrated by the moving rod (pp. 368–369 of [[purcellelectricity-and-magnetism-3rd-edition]]):**

| | Lab frame F | Rod frame F' |
|---|---|---|
| Rod | Moving with velocity v | Stationary |
| Cause of charge separation | Magnetic force **qv × B** | Electric force **qE'** |
| Field inside rod at equilibrium | Electric field **E = −v × B** | Zero (conductor screens E') |

- In **frame F**, charges inside the rod feel a magnetic force that pushes them to one end. Charges pile up until the resulting internal electric field exactly cancels the magnetic force — a [[steady-state-force-balance-in-electromagnetic-systems]].
- In **frame F'**, the rod is stationary so there is no magnetic force. Instead, a transformed electric field E' (from the Lorentz transformation of the fields) drives the redistribution, which the conductor then screens.

Both frames predict the **same charge distribution**, confirming that the physics is frame-independent even though the field decomposition into E and B components is not.

This relativistic perspective was historically one of Einstein's key motivations: the asymmetry in descriptions was resolved by recognizing that E and B are components of a single electromagnetic field tensor. See also [[motional-emf-and-charge-separation-in-a-moving-rod]].
