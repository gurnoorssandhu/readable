When a conducting rod moves with velocity **v** through a magnetic field **B**, the magnetic force **f** = q**v** × **B** acts on free charges inside the rod, driving them toward one end. This is the origin of motional EMF.

As charges accumulate at the ends, they create an internal electrostatic (Coulomb) field **E** due to surface charge redistribution. This field grows until it exactly opposes the magnetic force, reaching a steady state described by:

$$q\mathbf{E} = -\mathbf{f} = -q\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}$$

Dividing by q gives the steady-state condition:

$$\mathbf{E} = -\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}$$

This is not a new law but the force-balance condition rewritten. The rod behaves like a battery or capacitor: the magnetic force acts as the 'pump' separating charges, and the resulting electric field is the 'back-pressure' halting further separation.

Importantly, the electric field **E** here is *not* a Faraday induction field — it is a purely electrostatic field arising from separated surface charges, as confirmed in [[purcellelectricity-and-magnetism-3rd-edition]] (p. 369): *'the field in Fig. 7.3 is that of the surface charge distribution alone.'*
