This session focused on pages 358–372 of Purcell's *Electricity and Magnetism* (3rd edition), covering two closely related topics: motional EMF in a loop moving through a non-uniform magnetic field, and the relativistic interpretation of a moving conducting rod.

The reader first asked for a simple explanation of Equation (7.4), which describes the net EMF induced in a rectangular loop moving through a region where the magnetic field strength varies spatially. The assistant explained that only the two sides perpendicular to the direction of motion contribute to the EMF (the top and bottom sides contribute zero because the magnetic force is perpendicular to the path element there). The net EMF arises because the forces on the left and right sides of the loop are different — B₁ ≠ B₂ — so they do not cancel, yielding ε = v(B₁ − B₂)w.

The reader then asked about a passage describing two observers analyzing a moving rod in a magnetic field. The assistant explained the relativistic duality: an observer in the lab frame (F) sees a magnetic force qv × B push charges along the rod until an electric field builds up and balances it. An observer riding with the rod (F') sees no motion, hence no magnetic force, but perceives a transformed electric field E' that causes the same charge redistribution. Both frames agree on the final charge distribution even though they attribute it to completely different causes.

This session reinforced the concept of motional EMF as arising from magnetic forces on moving charges, and extended it to highlight that electric and magnetic fields are frame-dependent — a core insight of special relativity that is already implicit in Maxwell's equations.
