This session focused on pages 368–371 of Purcell's *Electricity and Magnetism* (3rd Edition), specifically the steady-state condition of a conducting rod moving through a magnetic field. The reader worked through the physical mechanism by which a moving rod develops an internal electric field and reaches equilibrium.

The discussion began with the overall logic of the steady state: as the rod moves with velocity **v** through magnetic field **B**, the magnetic force **f** = q**v** × **B** drives charge separation along the rod. Negative charges accumulate at one end, positive at the other, producing an internal electrostatic field **E** from the separated surface charges.

The reader then asked specifically about the nature of this electric field. It was clarified that this is *not* a Faraday induction field, but simply a Coulomb electrostatic field arising from charge redistribution on the rod's surface — analogous to a capacitor with separated charges on its plates.

Finally, the reader asked how the relation **E** = −**v** × **B** arises. This was derived by combining the steady-state balance condition q**E** = −**f** with the expression for magnetic force **f** = q**v** × **B**, and dividing by q. The result is not a new law but simply the algebraic statement that the electric and magnetic forces exactly cancel at equilibrium.
